A Story: Mira's Vending Machine

Story

Mira is 10. Her school just installed a snack vending machine. On day one, she puts in a coin, presses B4, and a chocolate bar drops. "Cool," she thinks. "It knew what I wanted."

But did it? Mira's older brother explains: "It didn't know anything. There's a list. B4 means chocolate bar. It's like a giant if-then sheet. If button = B4, then drop chocolate."

The next week, the school upgrades the machine. Now there's a camera. The machine looks at her face and says, "Hi Mira! Last time you got chocolate. Want the same?" That feels different. The machine seems to recognize her.

Then a year later, an even newer machine arrives. This one talks. Mira asks, "I'm feeling sad today, what should I get?" The machine pauses, then says, "Sometimes when I'm sad I want something warm. They have hot cocoa here. Want me to suggest that?"

Three machines. Three very different things happened. All three are sometimes called "AI."

This chapter is about untangling what people actually mean when they say "AI" — because it's at least three different things, and the confusion costs people a lot of headaches.

Three Honest Definitions of AI

When you hear someone say "AI," they're usually meaning one of these three things. They're related, but very different in what they can actually do.

AI is the broad field. ML is a subset of AI. Deep Learning is a subset of ML. Generative AI is a recent sliver of Deep Learning. They're nested like Russian dolls.

Definition 1: AI = "Machines doing tasks that seem to need intelligence"

This is the original definition, from the 1950s. By this definition, your spam filter is AI. So is the GPS that finds the fastest route. So is the auto-focus on your phone camera. They're all "doing something that used to need a human brain."

🧒 Kid Translation

If a machine does something that would impress your grandma — that's "AI." Your grandma would be impressed by GPS finding the fastest route. So GPS is AI. Even though it's just math under the hood.

Definition 2: AI = "Machine Learning — systems that learn from examples"

This is the more useful, modern definition. Instead of writing rules for a machine ("if email contains 'click here', mark as spam"), we show it examples ("here are 10,000 emails labeled spam or not — figure out the pattern yourself"). The machine builds its own rules from data.

🧒 Kid Translation

Imagine teaching a kid what a cat is. You don't say "a cat has 4 legs, fur, pointy ears, two eyes…" You show them 50 pictures of cats and 50 pictures of dogs and say "these are cats, these aren't." After a while, they can spot a cat they've never seen before. Machine learning is the same — but instead of a kid, it's a math equation.

Definition 3: AI = "Generative AI — machines that create things"

This is what most people mean in 2024–2025 when they casually say "AI." ChatGPT writes essays. Midjourney paints pictures. Sora makes videos. Claude debates philosophy. These systems don't just classify ("is this spam?") — they create ("write me a poem"). That's a fundamental shift.

🧒 Kid Translation

Old AI was like a really good sorter. New AI is like a really good storyteller. The sorter looks at things you give it and puts them in boxes. The storyteller makes new things that weren't there before — a poem, a picture, a video. Both are useful. Storytelling is newer and weirder.

When someone says "AI" today, ask yourself: do they mean the field, the technique (machine learning), or the thing that just generated their email? Almost always, they mean Generative AI without realizing it.

Rules vs Learning vs Intelligence

Mira's three vending machines correspond to three eras of AI. Understanding the difference is the foundation for everything else.

Three eras of AI. Each builds on the last. The transition from Era 2 to Era 3 happened in 2017 with the Transformer paper — Chapter 8 covers this.

How they handle "Is this picture a cat?"

Era How it works What it can & can't do
Rule-Based "Count fur pixels. If fur > 30%, count whiskers. If whiskers > 4 and ears are pointy, output 'cat'." Works on simple cases. Breaks the moment the cat is partly hidden, looks weird, or you've never seen this breed.
Machine Learning "Show me 100,000 images labeled cat/not-cat. I'll figure out which patterns matter." Excellent at this exact task. Useless if you ask it to also detect dogs unless you retrain it.
Generative AI "I learned how the world looks, broadly. I can spot a cat. I can also describe it, generate a new one, write a poem about it…" Surprisingly versatile. Sometimes confidently wrong. Costs much more compute.
Real example: Email spam

1990s spam filter: a list of suspicious words. If the email contains "Nigerian prince" or "click here now" — flag it.

2010s spam filter (ML): trained on a billion emails labeled spam or not-spam. Learned to weigh thousands of subtle signals — sender reputation, unusual link patterns, formatting quirks.

2024 spam filter (GenAI-augmented): can read the email's actual meaning and detect if a stranger is trying to manipulate the reader emotionally — even if no individual word is suspicious.

Narrow AI vs General AI

Another important distinction. Almost every AI you've ever used is "narrow" — it's good at one specific task. The dream (and fear) of "General AI" is something else entirely.

Every AI system that exists today is technically "narrow." Modern LLMs blur the line — they're general across language tasks, but still not general intelligence.
🧒 Kid Translation

A really good chess robot is like a kid who's amazing at chess but can't tie their shoes. They have one super-power and zero everything else. That's narrow AI.

A kid who can do school, sports, talk to friends, learn a new game, and figure out new things they've never seen — that's general intelligence. Computers can't really do that yet. But they're getting closer than they used to be.

💼 Pro Talk · Use these in conversation

ANI (Artificial Narrow Intelligence) — every AI shipping today. Sometimes just called "narrow AI" or "weak AI."

AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) — hypothetical AI that matches humans across all cognitive tasks. Sam Altman, Dario Amodei, and Demis Hassabis all talk about AGI as a goal — they just disagree on the timeline (2027? 2035? 2050?).

ASI (Artificial Superintelligence) — hypothetical AI smarter than humans at everything. The thing Nick Bostrom and Eliezer Yudkowsky write about. Don't bring this up casually unless you're at a Bay Area dinner party.

When someone says "We need AGI to solve this," they're usually wrong — most problems need better narrow AI, not general intelligence. Knowing this distinction makes you sound informed.

What AI Isn't (and Common Myths)

Movies have given everyone a pretty wrong mental model. Let's clean it up.

Myth 1: "AI is conscious / has feelings"

No current AI has feelings, awareness, or experience. ChatGPT saying "I'm happy to help!" is not happy. It's predicting that "I'm happy to help!" is the kind of thing that would be said in this context. Whether future AI could be conscious is a real philosophy question — but today's AIs are very, very fancy autocomplete.

Myth 2: "AI is always learning from your conversations"

By default, when you chat with ChatGPT or Claude, the AI does not update its weights from your conversation. The model is "frozen" at training time. Some companies use chat data to train future models — but the model you're talking to right now isn't getting smarter mid-chat.

Myth 3: "AI is one big brain"

There are hundreds of different AI models, made by different companies, trained differently, good at different things. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen — these are all separate models. They don't talk to each other. They don't share knowledge. Each one is its own self-contained system.

Myth 4: "AI = robots"

Almost no AI today is in a robot. Most AI is software running on servers. The robot in Terminator needed a body to be scary; ChatGPT just needs a web browser. The interesting AI stuff happens in data centers, not on legs.

Myth 5: "AI understands what it's saying"

This is the hardest one. AI seems to understand because its outputs are coherent. But "understanding" in the human sense — having a model of meaning, intent, truth — is a deep philosophical question, and most researchers think today's AI doesn't have it. (Some disagree. We'll come back to this in Chapter 11.)

⚠ Important

The fact that an AI is good at seeming intelligent doesn't mean it is intelligent in the human sense. This is called the Eliza Effect — humans naturally project minds onto things that talk fluently. Knowing this protects you from over-trusting AI outputs.
