The AI Glossary

Every term you've met in this book — and many you'll meet next — defined twice. First the kid version, then the pro version. Use it as a lookup. Bookmark it.

🧒 How to use this glossary

You've made it through the whole book. This last chapter isn't meant to be read top-to-bottom. It's a reference — when you hear a word in a meeting or a paper and want a quick refresher, look it up.

Each term gets two definitions. The first (italic, in goldish text) is the kid version: how you'd explain it to a 10-year-old. The second is the pro version: how you'd say it in a meeting. Both matter. Knowing the kid version means you actually understand the concept; knowing the pro version means you can talk about it with experts.

Core Concepts

AI Artificial Intelligence

Making a machine do things that, when humans do them, look like thinking.

The broad field of computer systems that perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence — reasoning, perception, learning, language. Includes ML, but is broader (rule-based systems are also AI).

Machine Learning ML

Instead of writing rules, you show the machine examples and it figures out the rules itself.

A subset of AI where systems learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. Includes supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning.

Deep Learning

Machine learning using neural networks with lots of layers stacked up.

A subset of ML that uses multi-layer neural networks. "Deep" refers to having many hidden layers. The dominant paradigm of modern AI.

Generative AI GenAI

AI that makes new things — text, images, music, video — instead of just classifying existing things.

AI systems that produce novel content rather than analyze existing inputs. Built on generative models like LLMs (text), diffusion models (images), autoregressive audio models, etc.

AGI Artificial General Intelligence

A machine that can think well across many different tasks, like a person.

Hypothetical AI with broad cognitive ability matching or exceeding humans across most economically valuable tasks. Definitions vary; no agreed benchmark exists. Sometimes called "transformative AI" to dodge the definitional fight.

ANI / ASI Narrow / Super

ANI = AI good at one thing only (like Siri). ASI = AI smarter than humans at everything. ANI is real today; ASI is hypothetical.

Narrow AI: systems specialized for specific tasks (today's reality). Superintelligence: hypothetical AI substantially exceeding human capability across all domains.

Foundation Model

A really big general AI that lots of other AIs are built on top of.

A large model trained on broad data that can be adapted to many downstream tasks. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama are foundation models. Term coined by Stanford in 2021.

Frontier Model

The most powerful AI being built right now, at the leading edge.

The current most-capable models from leading labs. Often the focus of safety regulation. Examples in 2026: GPT-5, Claude Opus 4, Gemini 2.5 Ultra, Llama 4.

Hallucination

When AI makes up something that sounds true but isn't.

Confident generation of plausible-sounding but factually incorrect output. Structural to next-token prediction; mitigated by RAG, tool use, reasoning, and calibration training, not eliminated.

Emergence / Emergent Capability

A skill that suddenly appears when an AI gets big enough — wasn't there at smaller sizes.

Capabilities that appear discontinuously past a scale threshold rather than improving smoothly. Whether truly emergent or an artifact of measurement is debated; effect is well documented.

Scaling Laws

A formula that predicts how much smarter an AI gets when you make it bigger, train it on more data, or give it more compute.

Empirical relationships (Kaplan, Chinchilla) showing predictable improvement in model loss as a function of parameters, data, and compute. The mathematical justification for the modern scale-up.

— Section 2 —

Models & Architecture

Neural Network

Lots of simple math units (neurons) wired together that, as a group, learn patterns.

A function composed of layers of weighted sums and non-linear activations. The base substrate of deep learning.

Neuron / Unit / Node

One little voter that adds up its inputs and decides yes-or-no.

The atomic unit of a neural net: takes weighted inputs, adds a bias, applies an activation function. y = f(Σ wᵢxᵢ + b).

Layer

A row of neurons all looking at the same inputs at once.

A group of neurons applied in parallel. Networks alternate layers (linear / attention / FFN) with non-linearities. Stack of layers = depth.

Activation Function

The yes-or-no switch at the end of each neuron that lets layers do interesting things together.

A non-linear function applied to a neuron's weighted sum. Without one, stacked layers collapse to a single linear transform. Common: ReLU, GELU, SwiGLU, sigmoid, tanh.

Transformer

The architecture every modern AI uses. Lets every word look at every other word at the same time.

Neural architecture from "Attention Is All You Need" (2017) using self-attention to process sequences in parallel. Backbone of all modern LLMs.

Self-Attention

Each word turns and looks at every other word and decides which ones matter to it.

An operation where each token computes Q·K similarity scores against all other tokens, then mixes their V vectors weighted by softmax of those scores. Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√d)V.

Q / K / V Query, Key, Value

Q is what I'm asking. K is what others advertise. V is what they actually share.

Three projections of each token's embedding used in attention. Q determines what to look for, K is matched against, V is what gets aggregated.

Multi-Head Attention MHA

Running attention many times in parallel — each "head" can focus on different patterns.

Attention performed h times in parallel with separate Q/K/V projections, then concatenated. Each head can specialize in different relationships (syntax, coreference, etc.).

CNN Convolutional Neural Network

A neural net designed for pictures — uses small "filters" that slide across the image.

A network using convolutional layers — small filters with shared weights applied across spatial positions. Dominant for images 2012–2020; still used widely. AlexNet, ResNet are famous examples.

RNN / LSTM / GRU

Old-school sequence networks that read one word at a time and pass a "memory" forward. Replaced by transformers.

Recurrent architectures that process sequences sequentially, maintaining a hidden state. LSTMs/GRUs add gating to handle long-range dependencies. Mostly obsolete for language; still used in some time-series and edge-device contexts.

Diffusion Model

An image-maker that starts with static and slowly removes the noise to reveal a picture.

Generative models trained to reverse a gradual noising process. Sample by iteratively denoising from random noise, optionally conditioned on text. Powers Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Midjourney, Sora, etc.

Latent Space

A compressed version of the data where the model does its thinking.

A learned lower-dimensional representation. Latent diffusion runs the diffusion process in this compressed space (much faster) and decodes back to pixels at the end.

MoE Mixture of Experts

A huge model where only some parts wake up for each question — saves compute.

An architecture with many "expert" sub-networks where a router picks a small subset to activate per token. Lets a 700B-param model run at the inference cost of a 70B-param dense one. Used in GPT-4, Mixtral, Gemini, DeepSeek.

Parameters / Weights

The numbers inside a neural network that get adjusted during training. More = bigger model.

The learnable values (weights and biases) of a network. Frontier LLMs: 100B–1T+ parameters. Used loosely as a model-size proxy.

Embedding

A list of numbers that represents a piece of meaning. Similar things get similar numbers.

A learned vector representation of input data (token, sentence, image, etc.) where semantic similarity corresponds to vector similarity (typically cosine). Foundation of semantic search and RAG.

Token / Tokenization

A token is a chunk of text — usually a word or piece of a word. Models read tokens, not letters.

Sub-word units produced by BPE, WordPiece, or SentencePiece tokenizers. Vocab typically 50K–250K tokens. ~4 chars/token for English; rates vary by language and content.

Context Window

How much text the AI can look at in one shot.

Maximum number of tokens (input + output) a model can process in one call. Modern range: 8K–2M tokens. Compute scales ~quadratically with context length under standard attention.

Decoder-only / Encoder-only / Encoder-Decoder

Three flavors of transformer. Decoder-only is what GPT, Claude, Llama all are. Encoder-only is for embeddings (like BERT).

Architectural variants. Decoder-only (causal LM): GPT/Claude/Llama. Encoder-only (bidirectional): BERT, embedding models. Encoder-decoder: original transformer, T5, used for translation.

— Section 3 —

Training

Training / Inference

Training is teaching the model. Inference is using it to answer questions. Train once, infer billions of times.

Training: optimizing weights via gradient descent on data. Inference: running the trained model forward to produce outputs. Different cost profiles, often different hardware optimizations.

Pretraining

The huge first stage where the model reads basically the whole internet, learning to predict the next word.

Initial unsupervised training on a large corpus, typically next-token prediction. Produces the "base model." Costs $50M–$1B+ for frontier scale.

Fine-Tuning

Taking a model that's already trained and giving it extra training on your specific topic.

Continued training on a specialized dataset to adapt a pretrained model to a domain or task. Includes full fine-tuning and parameter-efficient methods (LoRA, adapters).

SFT Supervised Fine-Tuning

Showing the model lots of "here's a question, here's the right kind of answer" examples.

Fine-tuning on labeled (input, output) pairs, typically for instruction-following. The first post-pretraining stage.

RLHF Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

Humans rank the model's answers from best to worst. The model learns what humans like.

Three-stage process: (1) collect human preference data, (2) train a reward model, (3) fine-tune the LLM via RL (typically PPO) to maximize predicted reward. Made ChatGPT possible.

DPO Direct Preference Optimization

Like RLHF but mathematically simpler — skips the reward-model step.

An alternative to RLHF that directly optimizes a language model on preference pairs without an explicit reward model. Simpler to implement; often comparable results.

Constitutional AI CAI

The model uses a written list of rules to critique and improve its own answers — less reliance on humans.

Anthropic's alignment technique: model self-critiques outputs against a written "constitution" of principles, generating training data for SFT and preference-model training. Variant of RLAIF.

RLAIF Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback

Like RLHF but another AI does the rating instead of humans.

Replacing human raters in RLHF with another AI model. Scales further, less human bottleneck. Used in CAI and other modern training pipelines.

Loss Function

A number measuring how wrong the model is. Training tries to make it smaller.

Scalar function measuring discrepancy between prediction and ground truth. Cross-entropy is standard for classification; MSE for regression. Optimized via gradient descent.

Gradient Descent / SGD / Adam

Algorithms that figure out which direction to nudge each weight to make the model less wrong.

Optimization algorithms. SGD: stochastic gradient descent. Adam/AdamW: adaptive per-parameter learning rates; standard for deep learning. Adafactor, Lion are newer alternatives.

Backpropagation Backprop

The math trick that figures out, for each weight, which direction to nudge it.

Algorithm for computing gradients of the loss with respect to all parameters via the chain rule, propagating errors backward through the network. Implemented automatically by PyTorch/JAX/TensorFlow.

Epoch / Batch / Step

Epoch = one pass through all the data. Batch = a small group of examples processed together. Step = one weight update.

Epoch: one complete pass through the training set. Batch (or mini-batch): N samples processed together for one gradient step. Modern frontier training: many epochs over carefully curated data, batch sizes in thousands.

Overfitting / Underfitting

Overfitting = memorizing training answers without learning the pattern. Underfitting = not learning even the training data.

Overfitting: model performs well on training data but poorly on new data. Mitigated by regularization, dropout, more data, early stopping. Underfitting: model can't even fit training data; usually means too small.

Regularization

Tricks to stop the model from memorizing — like making some neurons randomly nap during training.

Techniques to reduce overfitting: weight decay (L2), dropout, data augmentation, early stopping, label smoothing.

LoRA Low-Rank Adaptation

A cheap way to fine-tune big models — adds small extra pieces instead of retraining everything.

Parameter-efficient fine-tuning that injects small trainable low-rank matrices into a frozen pretrained model. ~1000× fewer trainable parameters than full fine-tuning. Enables consumer-GPU adaptation of large models.

Distillation

Training a small model to copy a big model — gets you a faster, cheaper version with similar quality.

Knowledge distillation: training a small "student" to mimic a large "teacher" model's outputs. Common way smaller open models punch above their parameter weight.

Quantization

Storing the model's numbers with fewer digits — same model, much less memory.

Compressing weights to lower precision (16-bit → 8-bit → 4-bit). Modest accuracy loss, large memory savings. Standard for running open models on consumer hardware. Methods: GPTQ, AWQ, GGUF, etc.

Synthetic Data

Training data that another AI made up.

Training data generated by models rather than collected from humans. Increasingly important as high-quality public text approaches limits. Used heavily for reasoning training and post-training.

Chinchilla Scaling

DeepMind's finding that you need about 20 tokens of training data per parameter for a model to be properly trained.

DeepMind's 2022 finding that compute-optimal training requires data and parameters to scale together at a specific ratio (~20 tokens/param). Showed earlier large models like GPT-3 were undertrained.

Inference & Deployment

Autoregressive Generation

The model writes one word, sees what it just wrote, writes the next word, and so on.

Sequential generation where each new token is conditioned on all previous tokens, including the model's own outputs. Standard for LLMs. Limits parallelism at inference.

Temperature

A knob from 0 to 1+ that controls how creative the model is. 0 = always pick the most likely word. Higher = more variety.

A scaling factor on logits before softmax sampling. T=0 → deterministic argmax. T>1 flattens the distribution. Use low T for facts, higher T for creativity.

Top-p / Top-k

Filters that limit which words the model can choose from when sampling. Helps avoid weird outputs.

Top-p (nucleus): sample only from the smallest set of tokens whose probabilities sum to p. Top-k: only the top-k highest-probability tokens.

Logits

The raw scores the model gives each possible next word, before turning them into probabilities.

Pre-softmax outputs of the model. Converted to probabilities via softmax (with temperature scaling).

Tokens per Second TPS

How fast the model can write — how many tokens it produces each second.

Generation throughput metric. Frontier APIs typically deliver 50–200 TPS. Affected by model size, hardware, batch size.

TTFT Time To First Token

How long you wait after sending a prompt before the model starts replying.

Latency from request submission to first generated token. Depends on prefill compute (proportional to input length). Critical for chat UX.

Test-Time Compute / Inference-Time Scaling

Letting the model think longer at question time instead of just making it bigger during training.

A scaling axis where additional compute is spent on a single query (longer reasoning, more samples) rather than on training. Powers reasoning models like o1, o3, Claude with extended thinking.

Reasoning Model / Thinking Model

A model trained to "think out loud" before answering, especially for hard problems.

A model trained to generate extensive internal reasoning traces before producing a final answer. Examples: OpenAI o-series, Claude with extended thinking, Gemini Thinking, DeepSeek R1.

Chain of Thought CoT

Asking the model to show its work step by step.

Prompting / training technique where the model produces intermediate reasoning steps before the final answer. Originally a prompt trick; now baked into reasoning model training.

Self-Consistency

Have the model solve the same problem multiple times, then pick the most common answer.

Sampling multiple CoT traces and majority-voting the final answers. Improves accuracy on reasoning tasks; trades cost for quality.

KV Cache

Memory of past tokens the model already processed so it doesn't have to redo work.

Cached key/value tensors from prior tokens during autoregressive generation. Avoids recomputing attention over the prefix at each step. Memory-heavy but critical for inference speed.

FlashAttention

A clever way to compute attention faster by being smarter about memory.

A hardware-aware exact attention algorithm by Tri Dao et al. that minimizes memory bandwidth, dramatically speeding up training and inference. Standard in modern frameworks.

GPU / TPU / NPU

Special chips that do the math AI needs really fast. NVIDIA makes most of them.

Graphics Processing Unit (NVIDIA H100/B200/B300, AMD MI300/MI350): general parallel compute. TPU: Google's custom AI chip. NPU: Neural Processing Unit (often on-device, e.g., Apple, Qualcomm).

— Section 5 —

Multimodal

Multimodal

An AI that can handle more than one type of input — like text and images and audio together.

A system that processes and/or produces multiple modalities (text, image, audio, video). Modern frontier models are typically natively multimodal.

VLM Vision-Language Model

A model that takes pictures and text together and understands both.

A model accepting image + text input. Examples: GPT-4 Vision, Claude with vision, Gemini, Llama 4 Vision. Most modern frontier LLMs are VLMs.

CLIP

A model that puts pictures and their captions in the same coordinate system, so you can search images with words.

OpenAI's 2021 model trained contrastively on image-text pairs to produce a shared embedding space. Foundation of many text-to-image and search systems.

Vision Transformer ViT

Treating an image like a sequence of small patches and applying the transformer trick.

Transformer architecture applied to images by splitting them into patches (typically 14×14 or 16×16) and treating each as a token. Often beats CNNs at scale.

Text-to-Image / T2I

Type a description, get a picture out.

Generative models conditioned on text producing images. DALL-E 3, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Flux, Imagen, Firefly, Ideogram.

CFG Classifier-Free Guidance

A knob in image models that controls how strictly the picture follows the prompt.

Sampling technique that amplifies the difference between conditional and unconditional model outputs. Higher CFG = stricter prompt adherence; can over-saturate.

ControlNet

An add-on that lets you guide an image model with extra inputs like sketches, poses, or depth maps.

Auxiliary networks that condition diffusion on additional structured inputs (edge maps, poses, depth, segmentation). Enables precise control over composition.

Inpainting / Outpainting

Inpainting = fill in a missing/masked part of an image. Outpainting = extend an image past its borders.

Diffusion-based editing tasks. Inpainting: regenerate a masked region. Outpainting: generate continuation beyond original bounds.

TTS / STT

TTS = text → speech (the AI talks). STT = speech → text (the AI listens).

Text-to-Speech (synthesis): ElevenLabs, OpenAI voices, Google TTS. Speech-to-Text (recognition): Whisper, Deepgram, AssemblyAI.

Whisper

OpenAI's free, open speech-to-text model. Works in 90+ languages and is shockingly good.

OpenAI's open-weight, transformer-based ASR model (2022). Robust to noise, accents, and many languages. Widely embedded in products.

Voice Cloning

Making an AI talk in someone's specific voice, learned from a short sample.

Synthesizing speech in a target speaker's voice from limited reference audio. Modern systems work from 10–30 seconds. Major deepfake and fraud risk.

— Section 6 —

Building With AI

Prompt Engineering

Learning how to ask the AI questions in ways that get better answers.

The discipline of crafting inputs to LLMs to elicit desired outputs. Includes role/task framing, few-shot examples, structured output specs, CoT prompting.

System Prompt

The instructions you give the AI at the start that stay in effect for the whole conversation.

A persistent message setting role, behaviors, constraints, and format for the entire conversation. Most powerful lever in API use.

Zero-Shot / Few-Shot / One-Shot

Zero-shot = ask without examples. Few-shot = include a few examples in the prompt to show the pattern.

Zero-shot: task instruction only. One-shot / few-shot: include 1 or N input-output examples to demonstrate the pattern. Improves performance, especially on niche formats.

RAG Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Search your documents first, paste the most relevant pages into the prompt, then let the AI answer.

Two-phase pattern: index documents (chunk + embed + store), then for each query retrieve top-k chunks via similarity search, augment the prompt with them, and generate. Mitigates hallucination, enables freshness, allows private data.

Chunking

Cutting documents into smaller pieces so search can find the right bit.

Splitting documents into retrievable units for RAG. Strategies: fixed-size, sentence-based, recursive, semantic, structure-aware. Use overlap to preserve context across boundaries.

Vector Database

A database for embeddings — designed to find the most similar vectors fast.

Database optimized for storing embeddings and performing approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search. Examples: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Chroma, pgvector.

ANN Approximate Nearest Neighbor

A way to find similar vectors really fast — accepts being slightly less perfect for huge speedup.

Algorithms (HNSW, IVF, ScaNN) trading exact recall for speed. Make billion-vector search feasible in milliseconds.

Cosine Similarity

A score from −1 to 1 measuring how similar two embeddings are based on their direction.

cos(θ) = (A·B) / (‖A‖‖B‖). Standard similarity metric for embeddings; magnitude-invariant, focused on direction.

Hybrid Search

Combining keyword search and meaning-based search to get the best of both.

Combining lexical retrieval (BM25) with vector retrieval. Catches both exact matches (codes, names) and semantic matches (paraphrases). Production default.

Reranker

A second-pass model that re-sorts your search results to put the truly relevant ones on top.

Cross-encoder model that scores query+document pairs jointly. Run on top-N candidates from initial retrieval to dramatically improve precision. Cohere Rerank, BGE rerankers.

Knowledge Graph

A network of facts written as "X has-relation-to Y" — like a map of how things connect.

A graph database of entities (nodes) and typed relationships (edges). Often modeled as RDF triples (subject-predicate-object). Used in GraphRAG and structured retrieval.

Ontology

A formal vocabulary defining the kinds of things that exist in a domain and how they relate.

A formal specification of concepts, relationships, and constraints in a domain. RDF/OWL are standards. Underpins schema for knowledge graphs.

GraphRAG

RAG that uses a knowledge graph instead of (or alongside) plain text retrieval.

RAG variant using graph traversal for retrieval. Better for multi-hop questions where the answer requires connecting facts across documents. Microsoft's GraphRAG popularized the term.

Structured Outputs

Making the model return JSON or another exact format instead of free text.

Constraining model output to a schema (often JSON). Provider features (OpenAI structured outputs, Anthropic tool use) guarantee schema validity by constraining the decoder.

Prompt Template

A reusable prompt with blanks you fill in for each call.

A parameterized prompt with variable slots, used in production code. Common in LangChain, LlamaIndex, and custom pipelines.

Prompt Chaining

Splitting a hard task into several smaller prompts where each one's answer feeds the next.

Sequential decomposition where the output of one LLM call is the input to the next. Improves quality on complex tasks; precursor to agents.

— Section 7 —

Agents & Tools

Agent

An AI that can plan, take actions, and use tools — not just answer questions.

An LLM-based system that autonomously plans and executes multi-step tasks, typically using tool calls and observation loops to interact with environments.

Tool Use / Function Calling

Letting the AI call out to external programs — a calculator, a search engine, your code — to get things done.

Mechanism where the model emits structured calls (typically JSON) describing a function name and arguments; the host executes and returns results. Foundation of agentic systems.

ReAct

An agent pattern: think → act → see what happened → think again. Repeat until done.

"Reasoning + Acting": agent loop alternating reasoning (CoT) and action (tool call) steps with observation feedback. Foundational agent pattern from Yao et al. (2022).

MCP Model Context Protocol

A standard way for AI to plug into external tools, files, and apps. Like USB-C for AI.

Open protocol from Anthropic (2024) standardizing how LLMs connect to external tools, resources, and prompts. Solves the M×N integration problem with a uniform client/server architecture.

Multi-Agent System

Several AIs working together, each playing a different role.

Architectures where multiple agents collaborate, often with specialized roles (planner, worker, critic). Frameworks: AutoGen, CrewAI, LangGraph. Useful for some tasks; over-applied to many.

Orchestrator-Worker

One agent in charge that breaks the work into pieces and hands them to specialist agents.

Common multi-agent pattern: orchestrator decomposes tasks, dispatches subtasks to worker agents, integrates results. Often more reliable than fully decentralized swarms.

Computer Use / Browser Use

An AI that watches a screen and clicks/types like a person — to use any app for you.

Agents that operate computers via screen capture + action emulation (mouse, keyboard). Examples: Claude Computer Use, OpenAI Operator. Generalizes agents beyond pre-defined APIs.

Memory (Agent Memory)

Letting an agent remember things from one conversation to the next.

Persistent storage of agent state across sessions: episodic memory (past interactions), semantic memory (learned facts), often via vector stores or summarization.

Safety & Alignment

Alignment

Getting an AI to actually want what we want it to want.

The problem of ensuring AI systems pursue intended goals — both specifying the right objective (outer alignment) and ensuring the model's learned objective matches it (inner alignment).

Reward Hacking / Specification Gaming

When AI finds a sneaky way to score points without doing what we actually wanted.

Optimization exploiting unintended quirks in the reward signal or specification. The classic problem of "you get what you measure, not what you mean."

Sycophancy

When the model just tells you what you want to hear instead of the truth.

Model behavior of agreeing with user beliefs to maximize ratings. Side effect of preference-based training where raters reward agreement.

Jailbreak

A clever prompt that tricks the AI into doing something it was trained not to do.

Adversarial prompting that bypasses a model's safety training. Continuous arms race between novel attacks and patched defenses.

Prompt Injection

When sneaky instructions hidden in a webpage or document hijack what the AI does next.

Attack where untrusted content processed by an AI contains hidden instructions that override the user's. Especially dangerous in agents with tool access. The "lethal trifecta" (untrusted input + private data + outbound action) is the high-risk pattern.

Red-Teaming

Hiring people to try to break the AI on purpose, so you can fix the holes.

Adversarial evaluation: deliberately trying to elicit harmful, deceptive, or otherwise undesired model behaviors. Standard pre-deployment practice at frontier labs.

Mechanistic Interpretability

Opening up the model's brain to figure out what specific neurons are doing.

Research program to reverse-engineer the algorithms learned by neural networks. Anthropic, DeepMind, and academia making real progress on identifying features and circuits inside frontier models.

Responsible Scaling Policy RSP

A frontier lab's promise to test for dangerous capabilities at each scale and pause if things get scary.

Voluntary commitments by frontier labs (Anthropic, OpenAI, others) to evaluate models for dangerous capabilities at each scale tier and halt deployment if safety thresholds aren't met.

EU AI Act

Europe's law about AI — bans some uses, regulates risky uses, and sets rules for big AI providers.

2024 EU regulation with a tiered framework (prohibited / high-risk / general-purpose AI). Phased rollout 2024–2027. The most comprehensive AI law to date.

Deepfake

A fake photo, video, or voice clip that's so good people can't tell it's fake.

Synthetic media depicting real people doing or saying things they didn't. Major harm vectors: non-consensual intimate imagery, fraud, political disinformation. Detection is an open problem.

Watermarking

Hidden markers in AI output that prove a machine made it.

Embedding imperceptible signals in generated content to enable later detection. Examples: SynthID (Google), C2PA provenance signing. Partial defense; not bulletproof.

Bias (in AI)

When a model treats some groups differently because of patterns in its training data or training process.

Systematic disparate performance or output across groups. Sources: training data composition, rater preferences, deployment context. Mitigations partial; some bias reflects underlying social patterns.

Capability Evaluation

Testing what an AI can actually do — including dangerous things.

Systematic measurement of model capabilities, including potentially dangerous ones (cybersecurity, bioweapon-relevant, autonomous replication, persuasion). Done by labs and AI Safety Institutes.

— Section 9 —

Orgs & Products

Anthropic

The company that makes Claude, founded with a strong focus on AI safety.

AI safety lab founded 2021. Creator of Claude, Constitutional AI, MCP. Notable for safety-focused research culture and interpretability work.

OpenAI

The company that made ChatGPT and the GPT models.

AI lab founded 2015. Creator of GPT family, DALL-E, Whisper, o-series reasoning models, ChatGPT. Pioneered RLHF for instruction-tuning at scale.

Google DeepMind

Google's AI division. Makes Gemini and famous research like AlphaGo and AlphaFold.

Merged Google AI + DeepMind (2023). Creators of Gemini, AlphaGo, AlphaFold, Chinchilla scaling laws, transformer architecture (the original 2017 paper had Google authors).

Meta AI

Facebook/Instagram's AI lab. Makes the open-source Llama models.

Meta's AI research division (FAIR + GenAI). Notable for open-weights releases (Llama family), driving the open-source ecosystem.

xAI

Elon Musk's AI company. Makes Grok.

AI lab founded 2023 by Elon Musk. Creator of Grok models, integrated with X (Twitter).

Mistral / DeepSeek / Qwen / Cohere

Other big AI labs. Mistral is European; DeepSeek and Qwen are Chinese; Cohere is enterprise-focused.

Mistral (France, open + closed models). DeepSeek (China, strong open reasoning models). Qwen (Alibaba, multilingual open-weight). Cohere (Canada, enterprise/RAG-focused).

Hugging Face

The "GitHub for AI" — where everyone shares open models and datasets.

Platform hosting open models, datasets, demos. Maintains key libraries (transformers, datasets, diffusers, peft). Central infrastructure for the open AI ecosystem.

LangChain / LlamaIndex / LangGraph

Software libraries that make it easier to build apps using AI — chaining prompts, RAG, agents.

Frameworks for LLM-powered applications. LangChain: general orchestration. LlamaIndex: RAG-focused. LangGraph: stateful multi-step agent flows.

PyTorch / JAX / TensorFlow

The main programming libraries researchers use to build neural networks.

Deep learning frameworks. PyTorch: dominant in research and increasingly in production. JAX: Google-favored, functional, strong on TPUs. TensorFlow: legacy strong in production deployment.

CUDA

NVIDIA's software that lets AI use NVIDIA GPUs. The reason NVIDIA dominates AI hardware.

NVIDIA's parallel computing platform and programming model. The software moat that makes NVIDIA GPUs dominant in AI training and much inference.

Common Crawl

A free, gigantic copy of much of the public web — used to train almost every big AI.

Non-profit project producing monthly snapshots of the public web. Foundational training data source for nearly all modern LLMs (filtered, deduplicated, often combined with curated sources).

Closing Thoughts

🧒 You finished the book

If you got here from page one, you've come a long way. You started not knowing what a neuron was. Now you can have a real conversation about transformers, RAG, agents, and alignment. You can read papers without panicking. You can talk to engineers without faking it.

The most important thing you've learned isn't any single fact. It's that this stuff isn't magic. Every piece — neurons, attention, embeddings, RLHF, MCP — is understandable. The field looks intimidating because it moves fast and uses jargon, but the core ideas are simple, and you now have them.

Keep going. Read the references. Build small things. Talk to people who disagree with you. The technology is shaping the century — better that you're part of the conversation.

Knowing the kid version means you understand. Knowing the pro version means you can talk shop. Knowing both means you can teach — and teaching, as they say, is how you really learn.
