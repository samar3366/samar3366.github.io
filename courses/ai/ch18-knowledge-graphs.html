Knowledge Graphs & Ontologies

Vectors capture similarity but not relationships. Knowledge graphs do the opposite: they encode which thing connects to which thing, and how. Together with RAG, they're how AI systems get factual rigor.

Kid Translation: A Web of Connections

🧒 Kid Translation

Imagine a giant family tree, but for everything in the world. Marie Curie is a scientist. Marie Curie is married to Pierre Curie. Marie Curie won the Nobel Prize in Physics. The Nobel Prize in Physics was first awarded in 1901. Each fact is a tiny arrow connecting two things.

Now imagine drawing all those arrows on one giant whiteboard. People, places, ideas, dates — every dot is a "thing," and every arrow is "how this thing connects to that thing." That's a knowledge graph.

If you want to ask "who did Marie Curie's daughter marry?", a knowledge graph can walk the arrows: Marie → daughter (Irène) → spouse (Frédéric Joliot). It doesn't have to guess. It follows the lines.

Vector search is great at "find me things similar to this." Knowledge graphs are great at "find me things connected to this, in this specific way." Different jobs.

Triples: The Atomic Unit

A knowledge graph is built from triples: three-part statements of the form (subject, predicate, object). Sometimes written subject — predicate → object. Each triple is one tiny fact.

— Triples —
(Marie Curie, born_in, Warsaw)
(Marie Curie, won, Nobel Prize in Physics)
(Nobel Prize in Physics, awarded_by, Royal Swedish Academy)

Stack millions of these together and the result is a graph: nodes (subjects and objects) connected by labelled edges (predicates).

Ontologies: Schema for Meaning

An ontology is the schema of a knowledge graph — the formal definition of what kinds of things exist and what kinds of relationships are allowed between them. "A Person can have a born_in relationship to a Place. A Person can have a parent_of relationship to a Person. A Place cannot have a parent_of relationship to anything."

Ontologies enforce consistency: instead of one source saying spouse, another saying married_to, and another saying partner, an ontology picks one canonical predicate and rejects the rest. This makes the graph queryable.

Three terms you'll meet:

  • RDF — Resource Description Framework. The standard format for triples, used across the web (and quietly powering Google's Knowledge Graph, Wikidata, schema.org).
  • OWL — Web Ontology Language. An RDF dialect that adds richer rules: a Father must be a Person, every Person has at most one date_of_birth, and so on.
  • SPARQL — the query language for RDF. SQL for graphs.

If you've never touched these, that's fine — most modern AI work uses simpler property graphs (Neo4j-style) where each node and edge can carry arbitrary attributes without strict schemas. But the underlying ideas — typed entities, typed relationships, queryable structure — are universal.

Knowledge Graphs vs. Vector Search

Vector search and knowledge graphs are good at very different things. The right system often uses both.

FeatureVector searchKnowledge graph
Best atFuzzy, semantic, "find similar"Exact, structured, multi-hop relationships
Building costLow — just embed textHigh — needs entity extraction, schema design
Query style"Find docs about X""Find all employees who report to people in Acme that joined after 2020"
Reasoning powerWeak — distance is just distanceStrong — can traverse, infer, aggregate
UpdatingRe-embed changed docsUpdate individual triples — surgical
ExplainabilityHard — why was this similar?Easy — show the path through the graph

The classic failure case for pure vector search: ask "who reports to whom in this org?" and the relevant facts may be spread across HR docs, org charts, Slack messages — and "reports to" doesn't necessarily appear as a phrase. A graph captures that structure once and answers it instantly.

GraphRAG: Combining Both Worlds

The hot architecture in 2024–2026 is GraphRAG: extract a knowledge graph from your documents, then use both vector search and graph traversal to answer questions.

  1. Build the graph from documents. An LLM reads each chunk and extracts entities and relationships into triples. Microsoft's GraphRAG, LangChain's LLMGraphTransformer, LlamaIndex KG modules all do this.
  2. For a query, retrieve from both. Use vector search to find relevant chunks. Use graph traversal to find connected facts. Combine.
  3. Use the graph to answer multi-hop questions. "Who funded the company that acquired X?" requires walking three hops — easy on a graph, very hard via vector search alone.
  4. Use community detection. Microsoft's GraphRAG clusters the graph into "communities" and produces summaries at each level. Lets you answer big-picture queries ("what are the main themes in this corpus?") that pure RAG can't.

The cost: building and maintaining a knowledge graph is real work. The benefit: dramatically better answers on questions that need structure or aggregation.

★ Practical advice

Don't build a knowledge graph just because it sounds sophisticated. Start with vector RAG. If users keep asking multi-hop questions ("who reports to X's manager's peers?"), aggregation questions ("how many incidents involved component Y last quarter?"), or "explain the path" questions — that's the signal to add a graph. Many production systems use a small, focused graph layered on top of vector RAG.

Vectors encode meaning as direction. Graphs encode meaning as connection. Modern AI systems benefit from both — vectors for "find me similar," graphs for "follow the chain."
💼 Pro Talk · How to sound fluent

"Triple" — subject-predicate-object. "Property graph" — graph where nodes and edges carry arbitrary attributes (Neo4j model). "RDF graph" — strict triple-store model.

"Ontology" — the schema. "Schema" — the same idea, less academic. "Taxonomy" — a tree of categories (a degenerate ontology).

"Entity extraction" / "NER" — pulling named entities out of text. "Relation extraction" — pulling triples out of text.

"Cypher" — Neo4j's query language. "SPARQL" — RDF's. "Gremlin" — a third option.

"Multi-hop reasoning" — answering questions that require walking multiple edges of the graph.

"GraphRAG" — the family of techniques combining knowledge graphs with retrieval augmentation.

"Knowledge graph completion" — predicting missing triples (e.g., we know X works_at Y and Y is_in Z, so probably X is_based_in Z). An active research area.

Self-Check Questions

🧠 Test yourself · Answer out loud
  1. What's a triple, and why is it the atomic unit of a knowledge graph?
    Answer

    A subject-predicate-object statement (e.g., "Marie Curie — born_in → Warsaw"). It's atomic because it's the smallest meaningful fact, and millions of triples compose into a graph that you can traverse and query.

  2. What's the difference between a knowledge graph and an ontology?
    Answer

    The graph is the actual data — millions of specific triples. The ontology is the schema — the rules about what kinds of entities exist and what relationships are valid between them.

  3. What kind of question is hard for pure vector RAG but easy for a graph?
    Answer

    Multi-hop relational questions. "Who are the children of people who worked at Bell Labs in the 1960s?" requires walking through specific typed relationships — vectors don't model that, graphs do directly.

  4. What is GraphRAG, in one sentence?
    Answer

    A retrieval architecture that extracts a knowledge graph from a document corpus and uses both graph traversal and vector search to answer questions, especially multi-hop and aggregate ones.

  5. When should you NOT build a knowledge graph?
    Answer

    When your queries are mostly "find similar content" or "summarize this topic" — pure vector RAG is simpler and sufficient. Graph construction is expensive; only do it when the queries benefit from structure.
