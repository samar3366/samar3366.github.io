Kid Translation: Three Schools of Training

🧒 Kid Translation

Imagine training a person to be a helpful assistant. You'd probably do three things, in order.

Step 1: General education. Have them read every book, paper, and article in existence. They'll absorb language, facts, reasoning patterns. They'll know lots of things — but they won't know what to do with that knowledge yet. They'll just continue any text you give them, because that's all they were trained for.

Step 2: Show them how to be helpful. Have them watch thousands of examples: "Here's a question, here's a good answer." "Here's a request, here's a polite reply." They start mimicking the assistant role.

Step 3: Polish. Have humans rate their answers. "Answer A is better than answer B." Over millions of comparisons, they learn what humans actually like — not just what's correct, but what's clear, helpful, and friendly.

Three stages, in that order. That's how you turn a giant text predictor into ChatGPT.

Stage 1: Pretraining (Read the Internet)

Pretraining is by far the biggest, most expensive stage. The model — starting from random weights — is trained to do one thing: predict the next token. Over and over, on trillions of tokens of text.

The training data is a curated mix:

  • Web pages (Common Crawl, filtered) — the bulk.
  • Wikipedia — overweighted because it's high quality.
  • Books — millions of digitized texts.
  • Code — GitHub. Surprisingly important for general reasoning, not just code skills.
  • Scientific papers — arXiv, PubMed.
  • Reference works — dictionaries, encyclopedias, Q&A sites.

The recipe is simple but expensive:

  1. Start with a massive transformer with random weights. Say, 70 billion parameters.
  2. Sample a chunk of text. Show the model the first N-1 tokens.
  3. Let the model predict the Nth token. Compare to the actual Nth token. Measure error.
  4. Backpropagate. Adjust weights to reduce error.
  5. Repeat ~10¹⁵ times. Yes, a quadrillion. Across thousands of GPUs for months.

The result is a base model. It's a brilliant text continuation engine — but it has no idea it's supposed to be an assistant. Ask it "What is photosynthesis?" and it might just continue with "is a question I'd love to answer in this essay" and keep generating an essay.

⚠ The cost

Training a frontier model costs $50M–$1B+ in compute alone. Plus salaries, data acquisition, infrastructure. This is why only ~10 organizations in the world train frontier models.

Stage 2: Supervised Fine-Tuning (Show It Examples)

To turn the base model into an assistant, we show it explicit examples of how an assistant should respond. This is supervised fine-tuning (SFT).

The training data here is much smaller — typically tens of thousands to a few million pairs, often hand-written or carefully curated. Each example looks like:

— SFT Example —
[USER]: Explain photosynthesis simply.
[ASSISTANT]: Photosynthesis is how plants make food from sunlight...

The model is trained to predict the assistant's response given the user prompt. The training method is the same as pretraining (next-token prediction), but the data is now structured as conversations and the dataset is small enough to be hand-curated.

After SFT, the model knows it's supposed to act as an assistant. But its answers might be verbose, evasive, or weirdly formal. Polishing comes next.

Stage 3: RLHF (Teach It What's Good)

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is what made ChatGPT actually pleasant. The idea: instead of writing perfect example responses, just show the model multiple options and tell it which is better. Humans are way better at comparing than writing.

RLHF is what makes the difference between a model that says "As an AI language model, I cannot..." ad nauseam and one that just helpfully answers your question. It's also where models get most of their "personality" and where alignment happens — for better or worse.

Constitutional AI: A Different Path

Anthropic (creators of Claude) developed a variant called Constitutional AI (CAI). The key idea: instead of relying entirely on humans to provide feedback, you give the model a constitution (a written set of principles) and have the model critique and improve its own outputs based on those principles.

  1. Generate a response. The model produces an initial answer.
  2. Self-critique. The model is asked to review its own output against the constitution: "Does this response violate any principles? How could it be improved?"
  3. Self-revise. The model rewrites its answer based on its own critique.
  4. Use the revised pairs as training data. Plus a smaller amount of human feedback for the parts the constitution can't capture.

CAI achieves much of what RLHF does with far less human labeling — and produces a model whose values are more transparent (you can read the constitution).

After Training: Distillation, Quantization, Fine-Tuning

After the big training stages, several other techniques shape the model that ends up serving you:

TechniqueWhat it doesWhy it matters
DistillationTrain a small "student" model to mimic a large "teacher" model.How a 7B-parameter model can punch above its weight. Many small open models are distilled from larger ones.
QuantizationReplace 16-bit weights with 4-bit or 8-bit. Tiny accuracy loss, ~4× memory reduction.Lets you run a 70B model on a single consumer GPU.
Fine-tuningContinue training the model on your domain-specific data.Make the model better at medical, legal, customer-service, or any specialty domain.
LoRA / PEFTLow-Rank Adaptation: fine-tune just a small number of extra parameters instead of the whole model.Cheap, fast fine-tuning. The standard approach for adapting open models.
Mixture of ExpertsArchitecture where only some weights activate per token. Trained from scratch this way.Massive parameter count without the inference cost. Powers many frontier models.
The model you talk to is the result of: (a) reading the internet, (b) being shown how to be helpful, (c) being polished by human comparisons, (d) optionally being shrunk and specialized. Each layer adds character.
💼 Pro Talk · How to sound fluent

"Pretraining" → "SFT" → "RLHF" — the three-stage pipeline. Sometimes "DPO" (Direct Preference Optimization) replaces PPO/RLHF; mathematically simpler, similar results.

"Base model" vs "instruct model" — base = pretraining only, instruct = post-SFT/RLHF. When downloading open models, you'll see both versions.

"Reward model" — the model trained to predict human preferences, used to provide a training signal during RL.

"Alignment tax" — the (sometimes-observed) phenomenon that RLHF makes models slightly worse on raw capability while making them more helpful and safer.

"RLAIF" — Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback. Like RLHF but with another AI doing the rating. Constitutional AI is one form of this.

"Synthetic data" — training data generated by other models. Increasingly important as human-written data hits limits.

Self-Check Questions

🧠 Test yourself · Answer out loud
  1. What's the difference between a base model and an instruct model?
    Answer

    Base models have only been pretrained — they predict next tokens but don't know how to act as an assistant. Instruct models have been further fine-tuned with SFT and usually RLHF to follow instructions and respond as a helpful assistant.

  2. Why is human ranking used in RLHF rather than human writing?
    Answer

    Humans are much better and faster at comparing two answers than at writing perfect ones. Comparisons also produce a richer training signal — they capture preferences that are hard to specify explicitly.

  3. What's the role of the reward model in RLHF?
    Answer

    It learns to predict human preferences from a small set of comparison data, then provides a scalable training signal for fine-tuning the LLM with reinforcement learning. It bridges sparse human feedback to high-volume RL training.

  4. How does Constitutional AI differ from standard RLHF?
    Answer

    It uses a written set of principles (the "constitution") and the model itself for critique-and-revise loops, dramatically reducing the amount of human feedback needed. The model's values become more transparent because they're written down.

  5. What's LoRA and why does it matter?
    Answer

    Low-Rank Adaptation: a fine-tuning method that adds small trainable matrices to a frozen pretrained model. Lets you fine-tune large models cheaply (often on a single GPU) and easily swap between specializations.
