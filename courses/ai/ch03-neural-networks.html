Neural Networks Explained

The actual structure under modern AI. Not as scary as it sounds — just lots of small math units cooperating.

Analogy: A Crowd Voting on a Decision

Story

A new principal at a school wants to decide: "Should we cancel today's outdoor PE class?" She doesn't decide alone. She asks 10 teachers. Each teacher gives an opinion based on different signals: the weather forecast, the kids' energy levels, the calendar, last week's pattern.

Some teachers' opinions matter more to her than others. The PE teacher's vote counts triple. The maintenance person's vote counts a little. The new substitute's vote barely counts at all. She sums up all their weighted votes — if the total goes above some threshold, she cancels PE.

That's a neural network — but with thousands of "teachers," each looking at a different aspect, each contributing a weighted vote, and the network slowly learning over time which teachers to trust for which decisions.

The "neural" in neural network comes from a very loose biological analogy — the human brain has ~86 billion neurons that pass signals to each other. Researchers in the 1940s wondered: could we make math units that work like simplified neurons? Stack a lot of them together, and could they learn?

The answer turned out to be yes. But the analogy to real brains is much weaker than people think — artificial neurons are very, very simplified. Don't worry about brain biology; worry about the math.

The Artificial Neuron

The basic building block. A single artificial neuron takes a bunch of numbers in, multiplies each by a weight, adds them up, runs the sum through a simple function, and outputs a number.

A single artificial neuron. Inputs come in, get multiplied by weights, summed with a bias, run through an activation function, and produce one output number.

Let's break down each piece:

  • Inputs (x₁, x₂, x₃) — numbers coming in. Could be pixel values from an image, words from a sentence, or outputs of other neurons.
  • Weights (w₁, w₂, w₃) — numbers that say "how important is this input?" These are what the network learns. Big positive weight = "this input matters a lot." Negative weight = "this input matters in the opposite direction." Near-zero weight = "ignore this input."
  • Bias (b) — an extra number added to shift the result up or down. Think of it as the neuron's "default mood" before it sees any input.
  • Activation function — a simple math function (we'll see in a moment) that decides "does this neuron fire or not?"
  • Output (y) — one number, which is then passed to the next layer of neurons.
🧒 Kid Translation

A neuron is like a kid deciding whether to go to recess. They look at three things:

(x₁) Is it raining? (Weight: 5 — really matters.)
(x₂) Did they finish their homework? (Weight: 2 — kinda matters.)
(x₃) Is their friend going? (Weight: 1 — small bonus.)

They add up: (rain × 5) + (homework × 2) + (friend × 1) + bias. If the total is high enough, they say "yes, recess!" That's the activation function: it converts the sum into a yes/no (or a probability).

"Learning" means: figuring out the right weights so the kid makes good decisions in many different situations.

Stacking Neurons into Layers

One neuron alone is pretty weak. The magic happens when you stack many neurons in layers, where the outputs of one layer become the inputs of the next. This is what "neural network" means.

A simple 4-layer feedforward neural network. Input (5 values) → 2 hidden layers (4 neurons each) → output (2 values). Real networks are much bigger.

The three types of layers:

  • Input layer — receives the raw data (image pixels, sentence words, sensor readings). No computation; just a holding place.
  • Hidden layers — the middle layers. They're called "hidden" because we don't see their outputs directly; they're just intermediate processing. This is where the network does its actual thinking.
  • Output layer — produces the final answer. Could be one neuron (e.g., "spam probability") or many (e.g., one neuron per possible category).

Why "deep" networks?

A network with many hidden layers is called deep. (That's where "deep learning" comes from.) More layers = more ability to learn complex, abstract patterns. GPT-4 has roughly 100+ layers. Modern image models have similar depth.

ⓘ Counting layers

By convention, the input layer is usually not counted. A "3-layer network" typically means 2 hidden layers + 1 output layer. Don't argue about this with anyone — different papers count differently.

Activation Functions: The Decision-Maker

The activation function is the small math function inside each neuron that decides what to output. Without it, a network of neurons would just be a fancy linear equation — boring and limited. The activation function adds nonlinearity, which is what lets networks learn complex patterns.

🧒 Kid Translation

Imagine a neuron that just adds up its weighted inputs and passes that exact number forward. If you stack 10 of these, the whole thing still acts like one big addition machine — it can only learn straight-line patterns. Boring.

Now imagine the neuron has a "decision rule": "if the sum is below zero, just say zero. If it's above zero, pass it through." Suddenly it can express curves, bends, weird shapes. That tiny twist — pass-through above, zero below — is called ReLU, and it powers most modern deep learning.

Three of the most-used activation functions. ReLU dominates modern deep learning because it's simple and trains fast.
Function What it does Where you'll see it
ReLU Negative → 0; positive → unchanged. Simple and fast. Hidden layers in almost every modern network. Variants: Leaky ReLU, GELU.
Sigmoid Squashes any number into the range 0 to 1. Output layer for binary classification ("is this spam? yes/no probability").
Softmax Squashes a list of numbers so they sum to 1 (a probability distribution). Output layer for multi-class classification ("which of these 1000 categories?").
Tanh Squashes to -1 to +1. Like sigmoid but centered at zero. Older RNN architectures. Less common now.
GELU Smoother variant of ReLU. Slightly better for transformers. Inside GPT, Claude, and most modern transformers.

Weights, Biases, and What "Learning" Actually Means

Now we can finally answer: what is a "trained model"?

A neural network is just a giant collection of numbers. Two kinds:

  • Weights — one per connection between neurons. A network with 1,000 neurons in one layer connecting to 1,000 in the next has 1,000 × 1,000 = 1 million weights, just for that one connection.
  • Biases — one per neuron (the "default mood" we mentioned).

Together, all the weights and biases are called the parameters of the model. When you hear "GPT-4 has ~1.7 trillion parameters" — that's how many weight + bias numbers it has.

"Training a model" = "finding values for all the weights and biases such that the model produces correct outputs on training data." The trained model IS those numbers — every other piece of code is just structure for using them.
Real example: Model file sizes

When you "download a model" (e.g., from Hugging Face), you're downloading a file containing all the weight numbers. Llama 3 70B has 70 billion parameters; the file is roughly 140 GB (each parameter takes 16 bits). That file is the model. Plug it into the architecture code, and you have a working AI.

Forward Pass and Backward Pass

Two phases of using a neural network. Both are crucial — and they happen in opposite directions.

Forward pass: data flows in, prediction flows out. Backward pass: error flows back, weights get nudged. Done millions of times during training.

Forward pass (also called "inference")

Data enters at the input layer, gets multiplied by weights, summed, activated, passed forward, layer by layer, until it produces an output. This is what happens every time you ask ChatGPT something — it's just a forward pass through ~100+ layers of neurons.

Backward pass (also called "backpropagation")

Only happens during training. Once we have a prediction and know what the right answer was, we calculate the error. Then we trace back through the network to figure out: which weights contributed to this error, and in which direction should they move to reduce it?

The math (chain rule of calculus) is intricate, but the idea is intuitive: blame is assigned proportionally to each weight that contributed, and each weight gets nudged to do better next time.

🧒 Kid Translation

Forward pass is asking the kid to spell "elephant." She writes "elefint." Wrong.

Backward pass is the teacher saying: "the 'f' should be 'ph' — that's the part you got wrong." Then the kid's brain adjusts the specific neurons responsible for spelling that sound. Next time, she'll try "eleph…"

The teacher doesn't say "rewrite the whole word" — they identify the exact part that was off, and the kid only adjusts that part. That's backpropagation. It's surgical, not global.

What Hidden Layers Actually Learn

One of the most beautiful results in deep learning: hidden layers spontaneously organize into hierarchies of features. Earlier layers learn simple patterns; later layers learn complex ones. Nobody programs this — it emerges from training.

The hierarchy of features in an image classifier. Edges combine into shapes, shapes combine into parts, parts combine into objects.

This emergent hierarchy is one of deep learning's most striking properties. The network discovers useful features at every level — without anyone designing them.

For language models, the hierarchy is similar but with different content: early layers learn syntax (commas, capitalization, common phrases), middle layers learn meaning (grammar, sentence structure), and late layers learn topics, style, and reasoning.

💼 Pro Talk · Network architecture vocabulary

Architecture — the structural design of a network: how many layers, how many neurons per layer, what activation functions, what connections. GPT, Claude, ResNet, U-Net are all architectures.

Parameters / weights — the actual learned numbers. "70B parameter model" means 70 billion of these.

FLOPs — Floating Point Operations. A measure of how much math the model has to do per forward pass. Big models = many FLOPs = more compute / more cost.

Feedforward network (FFN / MLP) — the most basic neural network architecture, where data flows in one direction (no loops). The kind we drew above. Also called a Multi-Layer Perceptron.

Hidden state — the activations of an internal layer. When people say "the model's representation of X," they mean the hidden state when you input X.

Self-Check Questions

🧠 Test yourself
  1. What does a single artificial neuron compute? Walk through the steps.
  2. Why do we need activation functions like ReLU? What would happen if we didn't have them?
  3. What's the difference between a forward pass and a backward pass?
  4. What are the "parameters" of a model? When someone says "70B parameter model," what does that mean?
  5. What patterns do early vs. middle vs. late layers tend to learn in an image classifier?
Show answers
  1. Multiply each input by its weight, sum them up, add the bias, run the result through an activation function, output one number.
  2. Without activation functions, the whole network reduces to a giant linear equation — it can only learn straight-line patterns. Activation functions add nonlinearity, enabling networks to learn complex curves and decision boundaries.
  3. Forward pass: data flows in, prediction flows out (used during inference and training). Backward pass: error flows back through the network, adjusting weights via backpropagation (only during training).
  4. Parameters are all the weights and biases in the network — the numbers learned during training. "70B parameters" means 70 billion of these numbers, stored in the model file.
  5. Early: edges, simple textures, color blobs. Middle: object parts (eyes, wheels). Late: whole concepts (cat, car, tree).
