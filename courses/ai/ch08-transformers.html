Story: A Classroom of Attentive Students

Story

Imagine a classroom of 20 students reading a sentence together: "The bank decided to give Maria a loan after reviewing her financial history carefully."

The teacher asks: "What does 'her' refer to?"

An RNN-style classroom would answer this by passing a note from student to student, left to right, each one adding context. By the time the note reaches the student looking at "her," it's been folded and refolded many times — useful information has faded.

A Transformer classroom does it differently. The student looking at "her" simultaneously asks every other student: "do you have anything that helps me figure out who 'her' refers to?" The student holding "Maria" raises her hand high. Others stay quiet. The "her" student aggregates the answers — most weight goes to "Maria." Done. One step. No noise.

That's the magic of the Transformer: every position can directly attend to every other position, in parallel, without information flowing through a long chain.

The Problem with RNNs

Before 2017, the standard for processing sequences was the RNN/LSTM. But these had two crippling weaknesses:

  1. Sequential processingRNNs process one token at a time, in order. You can't parallelize. On modern GPUs (which love parallelism), this leaves performance on the table.
  2. Long-range dependencies fadeInformation from early in the sequence has to pass through every subsequent step. Even with LSTMs' clever gating, signals from 100+ tokens ago weaken. Long documents are hard.

The Transformer solved both problems with one idea: attention. Process all positions in parallel. Let any position directly look at any other position. Distance no longer matters.

Tokens & Embeddings: Words as Numbers

Before we can talk about attention, we need to know how language enters the model. Two steps: tokenization and embedding.

Tokenization

Computers can't process letters directly. We split text into tokens — chunks that are usually 2–4 characters long, smaller than words. The sentence "AI is amazing" might become ["AI", " is", " amaz", "ing"]. Each token gets a unique ID number.

Modern LLMs use ~30,000 to ~200,000 unique tokens. The most common scheme is Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE), which builds the vocabulary by repeatedly merging the most frequent character pairs.

Real example: GPT-4 tokenization

The phrase "ChatGPT is incredibly cool" becomes the tokens ["Chat", "GPT", " is", " incredibly", " cool"] — 5 tokens. Common words become single tokens. Rare or technical words are split into multiple tokens. This is why your "tokens used" bill on the OpenAI API doesn't equal word count.

Embeddings

Each token ID is converted into a vector — a list of (typically 512–4096) numbers. This vector is called the token's embedding. Tokens with similar meaning end up with similar embeddings — "king" and "queen" are close; "king" and "banana" are far apart.

The embedding for each token is learned during training. Initially they're random; gradually the network discovers which tokens behave similarly and adjusts the embedding accordingly.

The pipeline from text to vectors. Tokenization splits the string into chunks; embedding converts each chunk into a high-dimensional vector of meaning.

Attention: The Big Idea

Now the heart of the Transformer. Attention is a mechanism for asking: "given my current position, which other positions should I look at, and how much?"

🧒 Kid Translation

Imagine you're reading a really long sentence and trying to figure out what one word means. You don't read every word equally — you focus more on the words that matter. "The dog ran fast because it was scared." When you read "it," your eyes flick back to "dog." You're attending more to "dog" than to "ran" or "fast."

Attention in a Transformer is the mathy version of that. Every word looks at every other word and decides "how much should I pay attention to you?" It assigns scores. Words that are more relevant get higher scores. Then the word's understanding is updated by mixing in the meanings of the words it's paying attention to, weighted by those scores.

Self-Attention in Detail

The specific form used in Transformers is self-attention — each token in a sequence attends to all the tokens in the same sequence (including itself).

For each token, the network computes three vectors:

  • Query (Q) — "what am I looking for?"
  • Key (K) — "what do I have to offer?"
  • Value (V) — "what's my actual content if you're interested?"

The attention score from token A to token B is the dot product of A's Query with B's Key. If they match well, the score is high — A pays attention to B. The scores get normalized via softmax (so they sum to 1). Then the output for token A is the weighted sum of all the Value vectors, weighted by these scores.

The attention formula
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q·Kᵀ / √d) · V

The √d in the denominator is just for numerical stability — it prevents the softmax from becoming too peaky when dimensions are large.

A simplified view of self-attention. Each token learns where to look. The pattern of attention reveals what the model thinks is grammatically important.

Multi-Head Attention

One attention pattern isn't enough. Different aspects of language need different patterns — grammar, meaning, reference, tone. So Transformers use multi-head attention: several attention computations running in parallel, each learning to focus on different aspects.

Modern Transformers have 16, 32, or even 96 attention heads per layer. Each head learns its own Q, K, V projections and attends to different patterns. The outputs are concatenated and combined.

What different heads learn

Researchers studying trained Transformers find heads specializing in different linguistic patterns: one head tracks subject-verb agreement, another tracks coreference (which "it" refers to which noun), another captures syntactic dependencies, another tracks topic continuity. These specializations emerge spontaneously from training — nobody designs them.

Positional Encoding

Self-attention has one weird property: it's order-blind. Without extra information, "the cat sat" and "sat cat the" would look identical to the model — it just sees a bag of tokens.

The fix: positional encodings. Each position in the sequence gets a unique vector that's added to the token embedding. Original Transformers used sinusoidal patterns; modern models often use learned positional embeddings or RoPE (Rotary Positional Embeddings).

RoPE has become the standard because it generalizes better to long sequences. It's how models like Llama and most modern LLMs handle position.

The Full Transformer

Now let's stack everything together. A Transformer block consists of:

A single Transformer block. Attention + FFN, with residual connections and normalization. Stack 12-120+ of these and you have a modern LLM.

The two key sub-components per block:

  • Multi-head self-attention — already covered. Tokens learn where to look.
  • Feedforward network (FFN / MLP) — a normal neural network that processes each position independently. Often described as where "knowledge" is stored — facts the model has learned end up baked into FFN weights.

Surrounding both: residual connections (the input is added to the output) and layer normalization (keeps activations stable). Both are crucial for training deep networks but conceptually plumbing.

Why the Transformer Won

Six reasons:

  1. Parallelizable. Unlike RNNs, Transformers process all tokens at once. Perfect for GPU training.
  2. Long-range dependencies. Any token can directly attend to any other. No information decay over distance.
  3. Scales beautifully. Performance keeps improving as you add parameters and data. Scaling laws (Chapter 7) work cleanly with Transformers.
  4. General-purpose. The same architecture works for text, code, images, audio, video, proteins, DNA. One architecture to rule them all.
  5. Pretrain once, fine-tune cheaply. A Transformer pretrained on the web can be adapted to many tasks with small follow-up training (Chapter 10).
  6. Interpretable-ish. Attention weights give clues to what the model is "looking at." Imperfect but useful.
The Transformer is the architecture that powers GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama, BERT, ViT (vision Transformers), and most generative image and video models. Different applications layer different things on top — but the core remains the same.
💼 Pro Talk · Transformer vocabulary

Self-attention — tokens attend to other tokens in the same sequence. Cross-attention — tokens attend to a different sequence (e.g., decoder attending to encoder outputs in translation).

Encoder-only (BERT, classification): bidirectional attention, no generation. Decoder-only (GPT, Claude, Llama): causal attention (each token only sees past tokens), used for generation. Encoder-decoder (T5, original Transformer for translation): both halves.

Context window — maximum tokens the model can attend over. GPT-3: 2K; GPT-4: 128K; Claude: 200K-1M; Gemini 1.5: 1M-2M. Longer is harder because attention is O(n²) in tokens.

FlashAttention — a memory-efficient implementation of attention. Used everywhere now.

KV cache — during generation, you reuse the K and V vectors of past tokens. This is what makes inference fast token-by-token. You'll hear "KV cache" all the time when people discuss inference performance.

RoPE (Rotary Positional Embedding) — current best practice for positional encoding. Encodes position via rotation in attention space.

"Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al. 2017) — the founding paper. Cite by full title to sound informed.

Self-Check Questions

🧠 Test yourself
  1. What were the two main weaknesses of RNNs that the Transformer solved?
  2. What's the difference between a token and an embedding?
  3. What do Q, K, and V stand for in attention, and how do they interact?
  4. Why do Transformers need positional encodings?
  5. Why is the Transformer architecture so general-purpose?
Show answers
  1. (1) Sequential processing — couldn't parallelize on GPUs. (2) Long-range dependencies faded as information propagated through many sequential steps.
  2. A token is a chunk of input (a word fragment, an integer ID). An embedding is the vector of numbers representing that token in the model's high-dimensional space.
  3. Query (what I'm looking for), Key (what I have to offer), Value (my actual content). Attention score = Q·K. The output is a weighted sum of all Vs, weighted by softmaxed scores.
  4. Attention is order-blind by default — it sees a bag of tokens. Positional encodings add information about each token's position so the model knows the sequence.
  5. It's just a sequence-to-sequence machine. Anything that can be tokenized — text, images, audio, code, proteins — can be fed in. The same core architecture works for all.
