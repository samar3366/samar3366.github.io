Reasoning Models & Test-Time Compute

Kid Translation: System 1 vs System 2

🧒 Kid Translation

Your brain has two modes. Fast mode: someone says "what's 2+2?" and you blurt out "4." No effort. Slow mode: someone asks "what's 17 × 23?" and you have to slow down, work it out on paper, double-check.

Until 2024, every LLM was a fast-mode model. It read your question, blurted out an answer in one shot. Sometimes brilliant, sometimes wrong — no real "stop and think."

Reasoning models flipped that. When you ask one a hard question, it generates pages of internal scratch work first — trying things, catching mistakes, trying again — and only then writes the final answer. The "thinking" can take 30 seconds or 5 minutes. The result is dramatically better answers on hard problems.

The terms you'll see: reasoning models, thinking models, extended thinking, chain-of-thought (CoT), test-time compute. They all point at the same idea: spending more compute at inference time to think harder, before answering.

Chain of Thought: Where It Started

The intellectual seed was planted in 2022. Researchers found that simply asking a model to "think step by step" before answering dramatically improved its performance on math and reasoning problems.

The original chain-of-thought trick

Without CoT:

Q: A juggler has 16 balls. Half are golf balls, half of those are blue. How many blue golf balls?

A: 4 (often wrong on similar problems)

With CoT:

Q: A juggler has 16 balls... Let's think step by step.

A: 16 / 2 = 8 golf balls. 8 / 2 = 4 blue ones. Answer: 4. (much more reliable)

Just adding "let's think step by step" as a prompt suffix improved math accuracy by 40+ points on some benchmarks. The model already knew how to reason — it just needed permission and space.

This led to chain-of-thought prompting as a technique. By 2023, every prompt engineer knew to ask models to "show their work."

What Changed in 2024

In September 2024, OpenAI released o1 — the first model where chain-of-thought wasn't just prompting, it was baked into training. The model would automatically generate long reasoning traces before producing an answer. Not because the user asked, but because that was its trained default.

The results were stunning. On math olympiad problems, o1 jumped from ~13% (GPT-4o) to ~83%. On competitive programming, it leapt into the top percentile. On scientific reasoning, similar jumps. The same architecture, but with reasoning-trained behavior, produced step-changes on hard problems.

How Reasoning Models Are Trained

The key insight: instead of training the model to imitate a final answer, train it to produce good reasoning traces that lead to correct answers. There are several ways to do this; here's the simplified picture:

  1. Generate many reasoning traces. For each problem, have the model attempt many different chains of thought, with random variations.
  2. Verify which traces produced correct final answers. Easy when answers are checkable (math, code).
  3. Reward correct traces, penalize wrong ones. Use reinforcement learning to update the model toward producing reasoning that leads to right answers.
  4. Repeat with harder problems. The model learns to plan, backtrack, double-check — behaviors that emerge organically when you reward correctness over long traces.

This works because for many problem types — math, code, logic — answers can be automatically verified. You don't need humans to grade; you just need a checker. That's why reasoning training has scaled so quickly: it's largely free of the human-rater bottleneck.

Test-Time Compute as a New Axis

The deeper insight from reasoning models: you can scale model performance by spending more compute when answering, not just when training. Up until 2024, every scaling story was about training. After 2024, there are two scaling axes:

AxisWhat you scaleEffect
Training computeHow big the model is, how much data it learns from.Better baseline ability across all tasks. Costly: $50M–$1B per run.
Test-time computeHow long the model "thinks" before answering. Token-level.Better performance on hard problems. Pay per query.

Reasoning models can spend 10–1000× more tokens "thinking" than a regular model would generate as an answer. For some problems this is wildly worth it — competitive math problems may take 50,000 tokens of internal reasoning. For "what's the capital of France," it's overkill.

Two scaling axes now exist: train bigger, or think longer. Both work. The smartest systems combine both — and increasingly, they trade off cost vs. quality dynamically.

When to Use a Reasoning Model

★ Use a reasoning model when

The problem requires multi-step reasoning: math problems, formal logic, complex code refactors, scientific analysis, planning, agent tasks with backtracking. Anywhere a wrong intermediate step compounds. Anywhere "I'm not sure" compounds across steps.

⚠ Skip reasoning models when

The task is fast, factual, or stylistic: writing prose, summarizing a doc, conversational chat, simple Q&A. Reasoning models are slower (30s–5min) and 5–30× more expensive per query. For "rewrite this email more politely," you don't need 10K tokens of internal thought.

Modern API designs increasingly let you control this: Claude has explicit "extended thinking" you can turn on with a budget. OpenAI's o-series has different reasoning effort levels. Use thinking when problems are hard; turn it off when they're not.

💼 Pro Talk · How to sound fluent

"Test-time compute scaling" or "inference-time scaling" — the new axis. Spending more tokens at query time for better answers.

"Chain-of-thought (CoT)" — the broader technique; reasoning models bake CoT into training rather than relying on prompting alone.

"Scratchpad" — internal reasoning the model produces but may not show the user.

"Self-consistency" — running CoT multiple times and majority-voting the answers. Often improves accuracy further.

"Process reward models (PRMs)" — reward models that judge intermediate reasoning steps, not just final answers. Used in some reasoning training pipelines.

"Tree of thoughts" — exploring multiple reasoning branches and pruning. A research direction beyond linear CoT.

Self-Check Questions

🧠 Test yourself · Answer out loud
  1. What's chain-of-thought, and what's the difference between CoT prompting and a reasoning model?
    Answer

    CoT is the technique of having the model generate intermediate reasoning steps before answering. Prompting CoT means you ask for it. A reasoning model has CoT trained in: it does long internal reasoning by default.

  2. What are the two axes of scaling, and how do they differ?
    Answer

    Training compute (bigger models / more data, costs $$$ once) and test-time compute (longer thinking per query, costs $ per query). Both improve performance; they're complementary.

  3. Why is reasoning training relatively cheap to scale?
    Answer

    Many target problems (math, code) have automatically verifiable answers — no human labeling required. The system can self-grade and self-improve via RL on verifiable rewards.

  4. When should you NOT use a reasoning model?
    Answer

    For fast, conversational, factual, or creative tasks where one-shot output is good enough. The latency cost (30s–5min) and dollar cost (5–30× more) aren't worth it for "summarize this paragraph."

  5. What's "self-consistency" and why does it help?
    Answer

    Running CoT multiple times with sampling and taking the majority vote on the final answer. It helps because each individual reasoning trace might make an error, but errors are rarely correlated — the right answer wins by majority.
